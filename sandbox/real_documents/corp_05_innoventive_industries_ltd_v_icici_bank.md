# Innoventive Industries Ltd. v. ICICI Bank
    
**Citation:** Civil Appeal No. 8337-8338 of 2017; (2018) 1 SCC 407
**Date of Judgment:** 2017-08-31
**Jurisdiction:** Supreme Court of India / Appellate Tribunals (Indian Jurisprudence)
**Bench:** Justice R.F. Nariman, Justice Sanjay Kishan Kaul
**Applicable Statutes:** Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) 2016 - Section 7, Section 238, Constitution Article 254

---

## 1. Introduction and Background Facts
ICICI Bank initiated CIRP under Section 7 of the IBC against Innoventive Industries. Innoventive argued that the proceedings were barred because the state of Maharashtra had suspended its debt liabilities under the Maharashtra Relief Undertakings (Special Provisions) Act. They argued the state law protected them from credit actions.

The dispute escalated when the adjudicating authorities passed their initial decrees, leading to appeals that eventually reached the highest appellate court. The case has significant implications for how commercial and real estate regulations are enforced across India.

## 2. Core Legal Issues Raised
Whether the federal IBC overrides state-level relief laws suspending liabilities under Article 254 of the Constitution and Section 238 of the IBC.

In resolving these issues, the courts had to balance the protection of individual investors and homebuyers against the commercial flexibility of lenders and promoters.

## 3. Arguments Advanced by Parties
### Arguments for the Appellants:
The debtor argued that the state relief act was active and suspended all legal proceedings for debt recovery. ICICI Bank argued that the IBC was a central law regulating bankruptcy and under Section 238 it has an absolute overriding effect on all inconsistent laws.

### Arguments for the Respondents / State:
The debtor argued that the state relief act was active and suspended all legal proceedings for debt recovery. ICICI Bank argued that the IBC was a central law regulating bankruptcy and under Section 238 it has an absolute overriding effect on all inconsistent laws.

The arguments centered heavily on the statutory interpretations of the relevant codes, and the legislative intent behind corporate insolvency and real estate consumer protection reforms in India.

## 4. Landmark Judgment and Detailed Analysis
The Supreme Court of India ruled that the IBC overrides all state laws that suspend debt liabilities. The Court held that under Article 254 (repugnancy) and Section 238 of the IBC, the central insolvency law prevails over state statutes. Once a default is established in a Section 7 petition, the NCLT must admit the insolvency application, and state-level moratoriums or debt suspensions cannot obstruct the process.

The Supreme Court re-emphasized that the primary objective of modern Indian commercial law is to ensure transparency, accountability, and the speedy resolution of defaults.
