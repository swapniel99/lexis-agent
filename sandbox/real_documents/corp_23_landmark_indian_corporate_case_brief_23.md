# Landmark Indian Corporate Case Brief 23
    
**Citation:** AIR 2023 SC 399
**Date of Judgment:** 2023-06-15
**Jurisdiction:** Supreme Court of India / Appellate Tribunals (Indian Jurisprudence)
**Bench:** Justice Sanjay Kishan Kaul, Justice Sudhanshu Dhulia
**Applicable Statutes:** Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) 2016 / Indian Companies Act 2013 - Corporate Precedent 23

## 1. Introduction and Background Facts
This landmark corporate dispute involves a major Indian conglomerate and issues concerning creditor rights and liability under the IBC. The corporate debtor defaulted on a financial facility of Rs 545 crores. The resolution professional filed an application under Section 43 of the IBC challenging preferential transactions executed by the directors just before the moratorium.

The dispute escalated when the adjudicating authorities passed their initial decrees, leading to appeals that eventually reached the highest appellate court. The case has significant implications for how commercial and real estate regulations are enforced across India.

## 2. Core Legal Issues Raised
Whether the transactions executed within the relevant look-back period constitute preferential transactions under the IBC and can be avoided by the NCLT.

In resolving these issues, the courts had to balance the protection of individual investors and homebuyers against the commercial flexibility of lenders and promoters.

## 3. Arguments Advanced by Parties
### Arguments for the Appellants:
The petitioner argued that the payments made to specific operational vendors were in the ordinary course of business to keep the company running. The lenders argued that the directors deliberately diverted funds to sister concerns, creating artificial operational debts to clear their own liabilities.

### Arguments for the Respondents / State:
The petitioner argued that the payments made to specific operational vendors were in the ordinary course of business to keep the company running. The lenders argued that the directors deliberately diverted funds to sister concerns, creating artificial operational debts to clear their own liabilities.

The arguments centered heavily on the statutory interpretations of the relevant codes, and the legislative intent behind corporate insolvency and real estate consumer protection reforms in India.

## 4. Landmark Judgment and Detailed Analysis
The Supreme Court of India, or NCLAT, held that the transactions were collusive and did not fall under the 'ordinary course of business' exception. The Court ruled that directors have a strict fiduciary duty to protect the assets of the debtor during financial distress. The NCLT was directed to recover the funds from the promoters and restore them to the corporate debtor's pool of assets for CIRP distribution.

The Supreme Court re-emphasized that the primary objective of modern Indian commercial law is to ensure transparency, accountability, and the speedy resolution of defaults.
