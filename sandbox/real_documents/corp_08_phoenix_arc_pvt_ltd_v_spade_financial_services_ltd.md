# Phoenix ARC Pvt Ltd v. Spade Financial Services Ltd
    
**Citation:** Civil Appeal No. 3063 of 2020; (2021) 3 SCC 475
**Date of Judgment:** 2021-02-01
**Jurisdiction:** Supreme Court of India / Appellate Tribunals (Indian Jurisprudence)
**Bench:** Justice D.Y. Chandrachud, Justice Indu Malhotra, Justice Indira Banerjee
**Applicable Statutes:** Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) 2016 - Section 21(2), Related Party Transactions

---

## 1. Introduction and Background Facts
During the CIRP of a corporate debtor, Spade Financial Services applied to be part of the Committee of Creditors (CoC). Phoenix ARC opposed this, showing that Spade was a related party of the promoter and had entered into collusive debt transactions. Spade argued it had ceased to be a related party at the time the insolvency process commenced.

The dispute escalated when the adjudicating authorities passed their initial decrees, leading to appeals that eventually reached the highest appellate court. The case has significant implications for how commercial and real estate regulations are enforced across India.

## 2. Core Legal Issues Raised
Whether a creditor who was a related party in the past but ceased to be one just before the commencement of CIRP can be excluded from the CoC under Section 21(2).

In resolving these issues, the courts had to balance the protection of individual investors and homebuyers against the commercial flexibility of lenders and promoters.

## 3. Arguments Advanced by Parties
### Arguments for the Appellants:
The appellant argued that related parties cannot be allowed to infiltrate the CoC by executing sham transactions to show they are no longer related. The respondent argued that the statutory language of Section 21(2) uses the present tense 'is a related party', meaning they should only be excluded if they are related *during* CIRP.

### Arguments for the Respondents / State:
The appellant argued that related parties cannot be allowed to infiltrate the CoC by executing sham transactions to show they are no longer related. The respondent argued that the statutory language of Section 21(2) uses the present tense 'is a related party', meaning they should only be excluded if they are related *during* CIRP.

The arguments centered heavily on the statutory interpretations of the relevant codes, and the legislative intent behind corporate insolvency and real estate consumer protection reforms in India.

## 4. Landmark Judgment and Detailed Analysis
The Supreme Court of India ruled that related parties must be excluded from the CoC even if they formally terminate their relationship just before CIRP. The Court held that the exclusion under Section 21(2) is meant to prevent promoters and their associates from hijacking the insolvency process. If a transaction is collusive or a related party relationship is terminated merely to gain access to the CoC, the NCLT has the power to look behind the veil and exclude them to protect genuine creditors.

The Supreme Court re-emphasized that the primary objective of modern Indian commercial law is to ensure transparency, accountability, and the speedy resolution of defaults.
